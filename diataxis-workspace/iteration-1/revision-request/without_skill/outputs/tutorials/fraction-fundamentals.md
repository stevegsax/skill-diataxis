# Tutorial: Your First Steps with Fractions

In this tutorial, we will build a solid foundation with fractions. By the end, you
will be able to read and write fractions, identify their parts, create equivalent
fractions, and simplify fractions to their lowest terms.

## What we will need

- A willingness to work through each step (no skipping ahead)
- Comfort with basic multiplication and division of whole numbers

## Step 1: The parts of a fraction

Let's start with a real situation. You buy a carton of 12 eggs and use 5 of them
to make breakfast. The fraction of eggs you used is:

```
  5
 ---
  12
```

Every fraction has two parts:

- **Numerator** (top number): tells us how many parts we have. Here, **5** --- the eggs we used.
- **Denominator** (bottom number): tells us how many equal parts make up the whole. Here, **12** --- the total eggs in the carton.

We read this as "five-twelfths."

**Check your understanding**: You have a bag of 8 marbles and you give away 3.
The fraction you gave away is 3/8. The numerator is **3** (marbles given away) and
the denominator is **8** (total marbles).

## Step 2: Reading fractions from a visual model

Imagine a pizza cut into 4 equal slices. You eat 1 slice.

```
  [ X ] [   ] [   ] [   ]
   ate   left   left  left
```

The fraction of pizza you ate is **1/4** (one-fourth).
The fraction remaining is **3/4** (three-fourths).

Now imagine a chocolate bar divided into 8 equal pieces. You eat 3 pieces.

```
  [ X ] [ X ] [ X ] [   ] [   ] [   ] [   ] [   ]
```

The fraction you ate is **3/8**. The fraction remaining is **5/8**.

**A money example**: A dollar is 100 cents. A quarter is 25 cents, which is 25/100
of a dollar. A dime is 10 cents, which is 10/100 of a dollar.

**Notice that** the denominator always matches the total number of equal pieces,
and the numerator counts how many pieces we are talking about.

## Step 3: Equivalent fractions

Suppose you have half a tank of gas. You could write this as **1/2**. But you
could also say the tank is at the 4 out of 8 mark, giving **4/8**. Both describe
the same amount of fuel.

Let's see this with numbers. Start with 2/4:

```
2/4  -->  (2 x 2) / (4 x 2)  =  4/8
2/4  -->  (2 x 3) / (4 x 3)  =  6/12
```

All three fractions --- 2/4, 4/8, and 6/12 --- are **equivalent fractions**. They
are different ways of writing the same amount.

**The rule**: We can create an equivalent fraction by multiplying (or dividing)
both the numerator and denominator by the same number.

**More examples**:

```
1/3  -->  (1 x 4) / (3 x 4)  =  4/12     So 1/3 = 4/12
2/5  -->  (2 x 3) / (5 x 3)  =  6/15     So 2/5 = 6/15
3/7  -->  (3 x 2) / (7 x 2)  =  6/14     So 3/7 = 6/14
```

**A practical example**: A recipe calls for 1/2 cup of sugar, but your measuring
cup only shows eighths. Multiply both parts by 4: 1/2 = 4/8. Measure 4/8 cup.

## Step 4: Simplifying fractions

Simplifying means finding the equivalent fraction with the smallest possible
numbers. We do this by dividing both parts by their greatest common factor (GCF).

**Example 1**: Simplify **6/8**.

1. Find the GCF of 6 and 8. Factors of 6: 1, 2, 3, 6. Factors of 8: 1, 2, 4, 8.
   The greatest common factor is **2**.

2. Divide both parts by 2:

```
6/8  -->  (6 / 2) / (8 / 2)  =  3/4
```

The simplified form of 6/8 is **3/4**.

**Example 2**: Simplify **10/15**.

1. GCF of 10 and 15: factors of 10 are 1, 2, 5, 10; factors of 15 are 1, 3, 5, 15.
   GCF is **5**.

2. Divide both by 5:

```
10/15  -->  (10 / 5) / (15 / 5)  =  2/3
```

So **10/15 = 2/3**.

**Example 3**: Simplify **12/18**.

1. Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12. Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18. GCF is **6**.

2. Divide both by 6:

```
12/18  -->  (12 / 6) / (18 / 6)  =  2/3
```

So **12/18 = 2/3**.

**Example 4**: You scored 15 out of 20 on a quiz. Simplify 15/20.

1. GCF of 15 and 20 is **5**.

2. Divide: 15/20 = (15 / 5) / (20 / 5) = **3/4**. You got three-quarters of the
   answers correct.

## What we accomplished

In this tutorial, we:

- Identified the numerator and denominator as the two parts of every fraction
- Read and wrote fractions from visual models and real-world situations
- Created equivalent fractions by multiplying both parts by the same number
- Simplified fractions to lowest terms by dividing both parts by their GCF
