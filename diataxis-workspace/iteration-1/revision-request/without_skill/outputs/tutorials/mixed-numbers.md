# Tutorial: Understanding Mixed Numbers

In this tutorial, we will learn what mixed numbers are, how to convert between
mixed numbers and improper fractions, and how to use mixed numbers in real
situations. By the end, you will be comfortable reading, writing, and converting
mixed numbers.

## What we will need

- Comfort with fraction fundamentals (numerator, denominator, equivalent
  fractions, simplifying). If you need a refresher, start with the
  [Fraction Fundamentals Tutorial](fraction-fundamentals.md).

## Step 1: What is a mixed number?

A mixed number combines a whole number with a fraction. You encounter them
constantly in daily life:

- A board is **2 3/4** feet long (two and three-quarter feet)
- A recipe calls for **1 1/2** cups of flour (one and a half cups)
- A baby weighs **7 3/8** pounds (seven and three-eighths pounds)

The whole-number part tells you how many complete units you have. The fraction
part tells you the leftover portion.

**Reading mixed numbers**: 3 2/5 is read "three and two-fifths." The word "and"
separates the whole part from the fraction part.

## Step 2: Understanding improper fractions

An **improper fraction** is a fraction where the numerator is larger than (or
equal to) the denominator. This means the fraction represents one whole or more:

```
5/4   -->  more than 1 whole  (since 5 > 4)
7/3   -->  more than 2 wholes (since 7 > 3, and 7 > 6)
12/5  -->  more than 2 wholes (since 12 > 5, and 12 > 10)
8/8   -->  exactly 1 whole
```

Mixed numbers and improper fractions are two ways to express the same value.
The number 1 3/4 and the fraction 7/4 represent exactly the same amount.

## Step 3: Converting a mixed number to an improper fraction

Let's convert **2 3/4** to an improper fraction.

The method: multiply the whole number by the denominator, add the numerator, and
place the result over the original denominator.

```
2 3/4  -->  (2 x 4 + 3) / 4  =  (8 + 3) / 4  =  11/4
```

**Why this works**: 2 whole units is the same as 8/4 (eight quarters). Add the
remaining 3/4, and you get 11/4 (eleven quarters).

**More examples**:

```
1 1/2  -->  (1 x 2 + 1) / 2  =  3/2
3 2/5  -->  (3 x 5 + 2) / 5  =  17/5
4 1/3  -->  (4 x 3 + 1) / 3  =  13/3
5 7/8  -->  (5 x 8 + 7) / 8  =  47/8
```

**A practical check**: You have 3 2/5 pounds of apples. That's 3 full pounds plus
2/5 of a pound. Each pound is 5/5, so 3 pounds is 15/5. Add the extra 2/5 and
you get 17/5. This matches the formula above.

## Step 4: Converting an improper fraction to a mixed number

Let's convert **17/5** back to a mixed number.

The method: divide the numerator by the denominator. The quotient is the whole
number; the remainder is the new numerator.

```
17 / 5 = 3  remainder 2
```

So **17/5 = 3 2/5**.

**More examples**:

```
11/4  -->  11 / 4 = 2 remainder 3   -->  2 3/4
7/3   -->   7 / 3 = 2 remainder 1   -->  2 1/3
25/6  -->  25 / 6 = 4 remainder 1   -->  4 1/6
19/8  -->  19 / 8 = 2 remainder 3   -->  2 3/8
```

**Important**: If the remainder is 0, the fraction converts to a whole number
with no fraction part. For example, 12/4 = 3 remainder 0, so 12/4 = 3.

## Step 5: Simplifying the fraction part

Sometimes after converting, the fraction part can be simplified.

**Example**: Convert 22/8 to a mixed number.

```
22 / 8 = 2 remainder 6   -->  2 6/8
```

Now simplify 6/8. The GCF of 6 and 8 is 2:

```
6/8 = 3/4
```

So **22/8 = 2 3/4**.

**Another example**: Convert 20/6 to a mixed number.

```
20 / 6 = 3 remainder 2   -->  3 2/6
```

Simplify 2/6 (GCF is 2): 2/6 = 1/3. So **20/6 = 3 1/3**.

## Step 6: Comparing mixed numbers

To compare mixed numbers, first compare the whole-number parts. If they are
equal, compare the fraction parts.

**Example**: Which is larger, 3 1/4 or 3 2/5?

The whole parts are both 3, so compare 1/4 and 2/5. Find a common denominator
(LCD of 4 and 5 is 20):

```
1/4 = 5/20
2/5 = 8/20
```

Since 8/20 > 5/20, we know **3 2/5 > 3 1/4**.

**Example**: Which is larger, 2 7/8 or 3 1/10?

The whole parts differ: 3 > 2, so **3 1/10 > 2 7/8** regardless of the fraction
parts.

## Step 7: Mixed numbers on a number line

Placing mixed numbers on a number line helps build intuition.

```
  |---------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
  0         1         2         3         4         5

  1 1/2 sits exactly halfway between 1 and 2.
  2 3/4 sits three-quarters of the way from 2 to 3.
  4 1/3 sits one-third of the way from 4 to 5.
```

**Practical example**: A road trip is 3 1/2 hours long. On the number line, that
is halfway between the 3-hour mark and the 4-hour mark. If you have driven for
2 3/4 hours, you have 3/4 of an hour left (since 3 1/2 - 2 3/4 = 3/4).

## What we accomplished

In this tutorial, we:

- Identified mixed numbers as a combination of a whole number and a fraction
- Converted mixed numbers to improper fractions using the formula (whole x denominator + numerator) / denominator
- Converted improper fractions to mixed numbers by dividing and taking the remainder
- Simplified the fraction part of a mixed number
- Compared mixed numbers by examining whole parts first, then fraction parts
- Placed mixed numbers on a number line
