# What Fractions Represent

## Why fractions exist

Whole numbers work well for counting discrete objects, but the world is full of continuous quantities: lengths, volumes, durations, shares. When you split a pizza among three people, no whole number describes each person's portion. Fractions were invented to describe parts of wholes — quantities that fall between the whole numbers on the number line.

The notation $\frac{a}{b}$ answers the question: "If I divide something into $b$ equal parts and take $a$ of them, how much do I have?" This is simultaneously a description (a count of parts relative to a whole) and an instruction (divide, then count).

## Fractions and division

A fraction $\frac{a}{b}$ is another way of writing $a \div b$. This is not a coincidence or a notational convenience — it is definitional. When we write $\frac{3}{4}$, we mean "the result of dividing $3$ by $4$." This connection to division is what gives fractions their computational power: every property of division transfers directly to fractions.

This also explains why division by zero is undefined. The fraction $\frac{a}{0}$ would mean "divide into zero equal parts," which has no coherent meaning. You cannot split something into no pieces and then count some of them.

## Why the same value has many names

One of the most important properties of fractions — and one of the most initially confusing — is that infinitely many fractions represent the same value:

$$\frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{4}{8} = \frac{50}{100}$$

This makes sense when you think about what fractions describe. If you cut a cake into $2$ equal pieces and take $1$, you have the same amount as if you cut it into $4$ equal pieces and take $2$. The pieces are different sizes, but you end up with the same total quantity.

Mathematically, the **equivalent fraction property** states:

$$\frac{a}{b} = \frac{a \times k}{b \times k} \quad \text{for any } k \neq 0$$

Multiplying numerator and denominator by the same number is equivalent to multiplying the fraction by $\frac{k}{k} = 1$. And multiplying any quantity by $1$ does not change its value. This is why the operation preserves equivalence — it is not a special rule to memorize but a direct consequence of what multiplication by $1$ means.

This property is what makes operations like addition with unlike denominators possible. When we convert $\frac{1}{3}$ to $\frac{4}{12}$, we are not changing the value — we are renaming it in a form that is compatible with another fraction we want to combine it with.

## The connection to decimals and percentages

Fractions, decimals, and percentages are three notations for the same underlying concept: parts of a whole.

A decimal like $0.75$ is a fraction with a power-of-ten denominator: $0.75 = \frac{75}{100}$. A percentage is simply a fraction with denominator $100$ written with a $\%$ symbol: $75\% = \frac{75}{100}$.

Some fractions convert to terminating decimals ($\frac{1}{4} = 0.25$), while others produce repeating decimals ($\frac{1}{3} = 0.\overline{3}$). Whether a decimal terminates or repeats depends on the prime factorization of the denominator — specifically, whether it contains only factors of $2$ and $5$ (the prime factors of $10$). Fractions whose denominators have other prime factors will always produce repeating decimals.

This is worth knowing because it explains why $\frac{1}{3}$ can never be written as an exact decimal. It is not a limitation of fractions but a limitation of the base-ten system. The fraction $\frac{1}{3}$ is perfectly precise; it is the decimal representation that approximates.
