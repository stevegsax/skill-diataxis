# Your First Fractions: Reading, Writing, and Visualizing

In this tutorial, we will learn to read and write fractions, visualize them as parts of a whole, distinguish between proper fractions, improper fractions, and mixed numbers, and convert between the last two. Along the way, we will work with concrete examples and check our results at every step.

## What we need before starting

You should be comfortable with whole number arithmetic: adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing integers.

## Step 1: Writing and reading a fraction

A fraction is written as two numbers separated by a horizontal bar:

$$\frac{\text{numerator}}{\text{denominator}}$$

The **numerator** (top) tells us how many parts we have. The **denominator** (bottom) tells us how many equal parts make up the whole.

Let's write the fraction "three out of four equal parts":

$$\frac{3}{4}$$

We read this as "three-fourths" or "three quarters." The denominator $4$ means we split something into $4$ equal pieces, and the numerator $3$ means we are talking about $3$ of those pieces.

**Check**: The numerator is $3$, the denominator is $4$, and together they describe $3$ out of $4$ equal parts.

## Step 2: Visualizing fractions as parts of a whole

Imagine a rectangular bar divided into $4$ equal sections. If we shade $3$ of them, the shaded region represents $\frac{3}{4}$.

```
[ ███ | ███ | ███ |     ]
  1/4   1/4   1/4   1/4
```

We can also place $\frac{3}{4}$ on a number line. Mark $0$ and $1$, divide the segment into $4$ equal parts, and count $3$ ticks from $0$:

```
0         1/4       1/2       3/4       1
|----------|----------|----------|----------|
                                 ^
                              you are here
```

You should see that $\frac{3}{4}$ sits between $\frac{1}{2}$ and $1$, closer to $1$.

## Step 3: Proper fractions, improper fractions, and mixed numbers

Now let's classify fractions into three types.

**Proper fraction**: The numerator is smaller than the denominator. The value is less than $1$.

$$\frac{3}{4}, \quad \frac{1}{2}, \quad \frac{5}{8}$$

**Improper fraction**: The numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator. The value is $1$ or more.

$$\frac{7}{4}, \quad \frac{5}{3}, \quad \frac{9}{9}$$

**Mixed number**: A whole number combined with a proper fraction.

$$1\frac{3}{4}, \quad 2\frac{1}{3}$$

Notice that $\frac{7}{4}$ and $1\frac{3}{4}$ represent the same quantity. We will convert between these forms next.

## Step 4: Converting an improper fraction to a mixed number

Let's convert $\frac{7}{4}$ to a mixed number.

First, divide the numerator by the denominator:

$$7 \div 4 = 1 \text{ remainder } 3$$

The quotient $1$ becomes the whole number part. The remainder $3$ becomes the new numerator, and the denominator stays the same:

$$\frac{7}{4} = 1\frac{3}{4}$$

**Check**: $1 \times 4 + 3 = 7$. The numerator matches, so our conversion is correct.

Let's do another. Convert $\frac{11}{3}$:

$$11 \div 3 = 3 \text{ remainder } 2$$

$$\frac{11}{3} = 3\frac{2}{3}$$

**Check**: $3 \times 3 + 2 = 11$. Correct.

## Step 5: Converting a mixed number to an improper fraction

Now let's go the other direction. Convert $2\frac{1}{3}$ to an improper fraction.

Multiply the whole number by the denominator, then add the numerator:

$$2 \times 3 + 1 = 7$$

Place this result over the original denominator:

$$2\frac{1}{3} = \frac{7}{3}$$

**Check**: $\frac{7}{3} = 2\frac{1}{3}$ (since $7 \div 3 = 2$ remainder $1$). It matches.

## What we accomplished

You have learned to:

- Write and read fractions using numerator and denominator notation
- Visualize fractions using area models and number lines
- Classify fractions as proper, improper, or mixed numbers
- Convert between improper fractions and mixed numbers in both directions
