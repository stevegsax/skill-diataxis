# Tutorial: Adding and Subtracting Fractions

In this tutorial, we will learn to add and subtract fractions step by step. We
will start with the simplest case --- same denominators --- and work our way up
to mixed numbers with different denominators. By the end, you will be able to
combine any two fractions confidently.

We will use a cooking context throughout: imagine you are combining measured
ingredients.

## What we will need

- Comfort with fraction fundamentals (numerator, denominator, equivalent
  fractions, simplifying). If you need a refresher, start with the
  [Fraction Fundamentals Tutorial](fraction-fundamentals.md).

## Step 1: Adding fractions with the same denominator

Suppose you pour $\frac{1}{4}$ cup of oil into a bowl, then add another
$\frac{2}{4}$ cup.

The denominators are the same (both fourths), so we simply add the numerators:

$$\frac{1}{4} + \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1 + 2}{4} = \frac{3}{4}$$

You should see that we kept the denominator at 4 and only added the tops.

**Result**: $\frac{3}{4}$ cup of oil in the bowl.

Let's do one more. You have $\frac{2}{7}$ cup of vinegar and add $\frac{3}{7}$ cup:

$$\frac{2}{7} + \frac{3}{7} = \frac{2 + 3}{7} = \frac{5}{7}$$

**Result**: $\frac{5}{7}$ cup of vinegar.

## Step 2: Why different denominators are a problem

Now suppose we need $\frac{1}{3}$ cup of water and $\frac{1}{4}$ cup of lemon
juice. How much liquid is that in total?

We cannot write $\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{2}{7}$. That would be wrong
--- we would be adding pieces of different sizes.

The rule is: **we can only add fractions when the pieces are the same size**,
meaning the denominators must match. We need a common denominator.

## Step 3: Finding the Least Common Denominator (LCD)

To add $\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{4}$, we need a denominator that both 3 and 4
divide into evenly.

List multiples:

- Multiples of 3: 3, 6, 9, **12**, 15...
- Multiples of 4: 4, 8, **12**, 16...

The smallest shared multiple is **12**. That is our LCD.

## Step 4: Converting and adding

Now convert each fraction to twelfths:

$$\frac{1}{3} = \frac{1 \times 4}{3 \times 4} = \frac{4}{12}$$

$$\frac{1}{4} = \frac{1 \times 3}{4 \times 3} = \frac{3}{12}$$

Now add:

$$\frac{4}{12} + \frac{3}{12} = \frac{7}{12}$$

**Result**: $\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{7}{12}$ cup of liquid.

Notice that we multiplied each fraction's numerator and denominator by the same
number --- this creates equivalent fractions (same value, different
representation).

**Second example**: You need $\frac{2}{5}$ cup of soy sauce and $\frac{1}{3}$
cup of sesame oil. How much liquid total?

1. Find the LCD of 5 and 3.
    - Multiples of 5: 5, 10, **15**, 20...
    - Multiples of 3: 3, 6, 9, 12, **15**...
    - LCD = **15**

2. Convert:

$$\frac{2}{5} = \frac{2 \times 3}{5 \times 3} = \frac{6}{15}$$

$$\frac{1}{3} = \frac{1 \times 5}{3 \times 5} = \frac{5}{15}$$

3. Add:

$$\frac{6}{15} + \frac{5}{15} = \frac{11}{15}$$

**Result**: $\frac{2}{5} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{11}{15}$ cup of liquid.

## Step 5: Subtracting fractions

Subtraction follows the exact same process. Suppose you have $\frac{3}{4}$ cup
of flour and use $\frac{1}{3}$ cup. How much is left?

1. Find the LCD of 4 and 3.
    - Multiples of 4: 4, 8, **12**
    - Multiples of 3: 3, 6, 9, **12**
    - LCD = **12**

2. Convert:

$$\frac{3}{4} = \frac{3 \times 3}{4 \times 3} = \frac{9}{12}$$

$$\frac{1}{3} = \frac{1 \times 4}{3 \times 4} = \frac{4}{12}$$

3. Subtract:

$$\frac{9}{12} - \frac{4}{12} = \frac{5}{12}$$

**Result**: $\frac{3}{4} - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{5}{12}$ cup of flour remains.

**Second example**: You have $\frac{5}{6}$ cup of milk and pour out $\frac{1}{4}$ cup.

1. LCD of 6 and 4:
    - Multiples of 6: 6, **12**
    - Multiples of 4: 4, 8, **12**
    - LCD = **12**

2. Convert:

$$\frac{5}{6} = \frac{5 \times 2}{6 \times 2} = \frac{10}{12}$$

$$\frac{1}{4} = \frac{1 \times 3}{4 \times 3} = \frac{3}{12}$$

3. Subtract:

$$\frac{10}{12} - \frac{3}{12} = \frac{7}{12}$$

**Result**: $\frac{5}{6} - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{7}{12}$ cup of milk remains.

## Step 6: Adding mixed numbers

You have $2\frac{1}{2}$ cups of broth and add $1\frac{2}{3}$ cups more. What is
the total?

**Method**: Convert to improper fractions, then add.

1. Convert each mixed number to an improper fraction:

$$2\frac{1}{2} = \frac{2 \times 2 + 1}{2} = \frac{5}{2}$$

$$1\frac{2}{3} = \frac{1 \times 3 + 2}{3} = \frac{5}{3}$$

2. Find the LCD of 2 and 3.
    - Multiples of 2: 2, 4, **6**
    - Multiples of 3: 3, **6**
    - LCD = **6**

3. Convert to sixths:

$$\frac{5}{2} = \frac{5 \times 3}{2 \times 3} = \frac{15}{6}$$

$$\frac{5}{3} = \frac{5 \times 2}{3 \times 2} = \frac{10}{6}$$

4. Add:

$$\frac{15}{6} + \frac{10}{6} = \frac{25}{6}$$

5. Convert back to a mixed number: $25 \div 6 = 4$ remainder $1$.

$$\frac{25}{6} = 4\frac{1}{6}$$

**Result**: $2\frac{1}{2} + 1\frac{2}{3} = 4\frac{1}{6}$ cups of broth.

## Step 7: Subtracting mixed numbers

You have $3\frac{3}{4}$ cups of stock and use $1\frac{1}{3}$ cups. How much remains?

1. Convert to improper fractions:

$$3\frac{3}{4} = \frac{3 \times 4 + 3}{4} = \frac{15}{4}$$

$$1\frac{1}{3} = \frac{1 \times 3 + 1}{3} = \frac{4}{3}$$

2. Find the LCD of 4 and 3: **12** (as we found earlier).

3. Convert:

$$\frac{15}{4} = \frac{15 \times 3}{4 \times 3} = \frac{45}{12}$$

$$\frac{4}{3} = \frac{4 \times 4}{3 \times 4} = \frac{16}{12}$$

4. Subtract:

$$\frac{45}{12} - \frac{16}{12} = \frac{29}{12}$$

5. Convert back: $29 \div 12 = 2$ remainder $5$.

$$\frac{29}{12} = 2\frac{5}{12}$$

**Result**: $3\frac{3}{4} - 1\frac{1}{3} = 2\frac{5}{12}$ cups of stock remain.

## What we accomplished

In this tutorial, we:

- Added fractions with the same denominator (e.g., $\frac{1}{4} + \frac{2}{4} = \frac{3}{4}$)
- Found the LCD to add fractions with different denominators (e.g., LCD of 3 and 4 is 12)
- Added fractions with different denominators using equivalent fractions (e.g., $\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{7}{12}$)
- Subtracted fractions using the same LCD method (e.g., $\frac{3}{4} - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{5}{12}$)
- Added mixed numbers by converting to improper fractions first (e.g., $2\frac{1}{2} + 1\frac{2}{3} = 4\frac{1}{6}$)
- Subtracted mixed numbers using the same approach (e.g., $3\frac{3}{4} - 1\frac{1}{3} = 2\frac{5}{12}$)
