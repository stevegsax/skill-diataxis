# Tutorial: Your First Steps with Fractions

In this tutorial, we will build a solid foundation with fractions. By the end, you
will be able to read and write fractions, identify their parts, create equivalent
fractions, simplify fractions to their lowest terms, and work with mixed numbers.

## What we will need

- A willingness to work through each step (no skipping ahead)
- Comfort with basic multiplication and division of whole numbers

## Step 1: The parts of a fraction

Let's start with a concrete fraction. Imagine you cut a pizza into 4 equal slices
and take 3 of them. The fraction representing what you took is:

$$\frac{3}{4}$$

Every fraction has two parts:

- **Numerator** (top number): tells us how many parts we have. Here, **3**.
- **Denominator** (bottom number): tells us how many equal parts make up the whole. Here, **4**.

We read $\frac{3}{4}$ as "three-fourths" or "three quarters."

**Check your understanding**: In the fraction $\frac{5}{8}$, the numerator is **5** and the
denominator is **8**. We have 5 parts out of 8 equal parts.

## Step 2: Reading fractions from a visual model

Imagine a pizza cut into 4 equal slices. You eat 1 slice.

```
  [ X ] [   ] [   ] [   ]
   ate   left   left  left
```

The fraction of pizza you ate is $\frac{1}{4}$ (one-fourth).
The fraction remaining is $\frac{3}{4}$ (three-fourths).

Now imagine a pizza cut into 8 equal slices. You eat 3 slices.

```
  [ X ] [ X ] [ X ] [   ] [   ] [   ] [   ] [   ]
```

The fraction you ate is $\frac{3}{8}$. The fraction remaining is $\frac{5}{8}$.

**Notice that** the denominator always matches the total number of equal pieces,
and the numerator counts how many pieces we are talking about.

## Step 3: Equivalent fractions

Let's work with specific numbers. Take a pizza cut into 4 slices and eat 2 of them:

```
  [ X ] [ X ] [   ] [   ]       -->  2/4
```

Now imagine cutting each of those 4 slices in half, giving us 8 slices total.
The amount of pizza eaten has not changed, but now we can describe it differently:

```
  [X] [X] [X] [X] [ ] [ ] [ ] [ ]   -->  4/8
```

We have just discovered that $\frac{2}{4} = \frac{4}{8}$. These are **equivalent fractions** ---
different ways of writing the same amount.

**The rule**: We can create an equivalent fraction by multiplying (or dividing)
both the numerator and denominator by the same number.

Let's verify with the actual arithmetic:

$$\frac{2}{4} = \frac{2 \times 2}{4 \times 2} = \frac{4}{8}$$

You should see that we multiplied both the top and bottom by 2.

Let's try another. Starting from $\frac{1}{3}$, multiply both parts by 4:

$$\frac{1}{3} = \frac{1 \times 4}{3 \times 4} = \frac{4}{12}$$

So $\frac{1}{3} = \frac{4}{12}$. The value did not change because we multiplied top and bottom by the same number.

One more: starting from $\frac{2}{5}$, multiply both parts by 3:

$$\frac{2}{5} = \frac{2 \times 3}{5 \times 3} = \frac{6}{15}$$

So $\frac{2}{5} = \frac{6}{15}$.

## Step 4: Simplifying fractions

Simplifying means finding the equivalent fraction with the smallest possible
numbers. We do this by dividing both parts by their greatest common factor (GCF).

**Example 1**: Simplify $\frac{6}{8}$.

1. Find the GCF of 6 and 8.
    - Factors of 6: 1, 2, 3, 6
    - Factors of 8: 1, 2, 4, 8
    - The greatest common factor is **2**.

2. Divide both parts by 2:

$$\frac{6}{8} = \frac{6 \div 2}{8 \div 2} = \frac{3}{4}$$

The simplified form of $\frac{6}{8}$ is $\frac{3}{4}$.

**Example 2**: Simplify $\frac{10}{15}$.

1. Find the GCF of 10 and 15.
    - Factors of 10: 1, 2, 5, 10
    - Factors of 15: 1, 3, 5, 15
    - GCF is **5**.

2. Divide both by 5:

$$\frac{10}{15} = \frac{10 \div 5}{15 \div 5} = \frac{2}{3}$$

You should see that $\frac{10}{15} = \frac{2}{3}$.

**Example 3**: Simplify $\frac{12}{18}$.

1. Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12. Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18. GCF is **6**.

2. Divide both by 6:

$$\frac{12}{18} = \frac{12 \div 6}{18 \div 6} = \frac{2}{3}$$

## Step 5: Mixed numbers

So far, all of our fractions have been less than 1 (the numerator was smaller than
the denominator). But what happens when you have more than a whole?

Suppose you eat an entire pizza (8 slices out of 8) and then 3 slices from a
second pizza. You have eaten $\frac{11}{8}$ of a pizza. This is called an
**improper fraction** --- the numerator is larger than the denominator.

A **mixed number** is another way to write the same amount: $1\frac{3}{8}$. It
combines a whole number part (1) with a proper fraction part ($\frac{3}{8}$).

### Converting an improper fraction to a mixed number

Let's convert $\frac{7}{3}$ to a mixed number.

1. Divide the numerator by the denominator: $7 \div 3 = 2$ remainder $1$.
2. The quotient (2) is the whole number part.
3. The remainder (1) is the new numerator, and the denominator stays the same.

$$\frac{7}{3} = 2\frac{1}{3}$$

**Another example**: Convert $\frac{17}{5}$.

1. $17 \div 5 = 3$ remainder $2$.

$$\frac{17}{5} = 3\frac{2}{5}$$

### Converting a mixed number to an improper fraction

Let's convert $2\frac{3}{4}$ to an improper fraction.

1. Multiply the whole number by the denominator: $2 \times 4 = 8$.
2. Add the numerator: $8 + 3 = 11$.
3. Keep the same denominator.

$$2\frac{3}{4} = \frac{2 \times 4 + 3}{4} = \frac{11}{4}$$

**Another example**: Convert $3\frac{2}{5}$.

1. $3 \times 5 = 15$
2. $15 + 2 = 17$

$$3\frac{2}{5} = \frac{17}{5}$$

You should see that these two conversions are inverses of each other --- we
converted $\frac{17}{5}$ to $3\frac{2}{5}$ above, and now we have confirmed
that $3\frac{2}{5}$ converts back to $\frac{17}{5}$.

## What we accomplished

In this tutorial, we:

- Identified the numerator and denominator as the two parts of every fraction
- Read and wrote fractions from visual models (e.g., $\frac{3}{8}$ of a pizza)
- Created equivalent fractions by multiplying both parts by the same number
  (e.g., $\frac{1}{3} = \frac{4}{12}$)
- Simplified fractions to lowest terms by dividing both parts by their GCF
  (e.g., $\frac{12}{18} = \frac{2}{3}$)
- Learned what mixed numbers are and how to convert between mixed numbers and
  improper fractions (e.g., $\frac{7}{3} = 2\frac{1}{3}$)
