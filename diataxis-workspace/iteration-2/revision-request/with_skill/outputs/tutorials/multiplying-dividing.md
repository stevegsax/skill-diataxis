# Tutorial: Multiplying and Dividing Fractions

In this tutorial, we will learn to multiply and divide fractions. The good news:
multiplication is actually simpler than addition (no common denominators needed).
Division has one extra step but builds directly on multiplication. By the end,
you will be able to handle both operations with fractions and mixed numbers.

We will use recipe scaling as our running example.

## What we will need

- Comfort with fraction fundamentals and simplifying. If needed, review the
  [Fraction Fundamentals Tutorial](fraction-fundamentals.md).

## Step 1: Multiplying two fractions

A recipe calls for $\frac{2}{3}$ cup of rice. You want to make half the recipe.
How much rice do you need?

"Half of $\frac{2}{3}$" means $\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{2}{3}$.

To multiply fractions: **multiply the numerators, multiply the denominators**.

$$\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{2}{3} = \frac{1 \times 2}{2 \times 3} = \frac{2}{6}$$

Now simplify. The GCF of 2 and 6 is 2:

$$\frac{2}{6} = \frac{2 \div 2}{6 \div 2} = \frac{1}{3}$$

**Result**: You need $\frac{1}{3}$ cup of rice. Notice that multiplying by
$\frac{1}{2}$ gave us a *smaller* result --- half of $\frac{2}{3}$ is $\frac{1}{3}$.
This makes sense: taking a fraction of a fraction gives a smaller piece.

**Second example**: A sauce needs $\frac{3}{4}$ cup of cream. You want to make
$\frac{2}{5}$ of the recipe.

$$\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{5} = \frac{3 \times 2}{4 \times 5} = \frac{6}{20}$$

Simplify. GCF of 6 and 20 is 2:

$$\frac{6}{20} = \frac{6 \div 2}{20 \div 2} = \frac{3}{10}$$

**Result**: You need $\frac{3}{10}$ cup of cream.

## Step 2: Multiplying a fraction by a whole number

The recipe calls for $\frac{3}{4}$ teaspoon of salt. You want to triple it.

Write 3 as a fraction: $\frac{3}{1}$. Then multiply:

$$\frac{3}{1} \times \frac{3}{4} = \frac{3 \times 3}{1 \times 4} = \frac{9}{4}$$

Convert to a mixed number: $9 \div 4 = 2$ remainder $1$.

$$\frac{9}{4} = 2\frac{1}{4}$$

**Result**: You need $2\frac{1}{4}$ teaspoons of salt.

**Second example**: A recipe uses $\frac{2}{5}$ cup of honey. You want to make
4 batches.

$$\frac{4}{1} \times \frac{2}{5} = \frac{4 \times 2}{1 \times 5} = \frac{8}{5}$$

Convert: $8 \div 5 = 1$ remainder $3$.

$$\frac{8}{5} = 1\frac{3}{5}$$

**Result**: You need $1\frac{3}{5}$ cups of honey for 4 batches.

## Step 3: Understanding reciprocals

Before we can divide, we need to understand reciprocals.

The **reciprocal** of a fraction is that fraction flipped upside down:

- Reciprocal of $\frac{3}{4}$ is $\frac{4}{3}$
- Reciprocal of $\frac{2}{5}$ is $\frac{5}{2}$
- Reciprocal of $\frac{7}{1}$ (which is just 7) is $\frac{1}{7}$

A key property: any number multiplied by its reciprocal equals 1. Let's verify
with $\frac{3}{4}$:

$$\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{4}{3} = \frac{3 \times 4}{4 \times 3} = \frac{12}{12} = 1$$

You should see that the 3s and 4s appear in both positions, so everything cancels.

## Step 4: Dividing fractions

You have $\frac{3}{4}$ cup of sugar and want to split it equally into 2
containers. How much goes in each?

This is $\frac{3}{4} \div 2$.

The rule for dividing fractions: **multiply by the reciprocal of the divisor**.

1. Write 2 as a fraction: $\frac{2}{1}$
2. Find its reciprocal: $\frac{1}{2}$
3. Multiply:

$$\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{3 \times 1}{4 \times 2} = \frac{3}{8}$$

**Result**: Each container gets $\frac{3}{8}$ cup of sugar.

**Now let's try fraction-by-fraction**: Compute $\frac{2}{3} \div \frac{4}{5}$.

1. Reciprocal of $\frac{4}{5}$ is $\frac{5}{4}$.
2. Multiply:

$$\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{5}{4} = \frac{2 \times 5}{3 \times 4} = \frac{10}{12}$$

3. Simplify. GCF of 10 and 12 is 2:

$$\frac{10}{12} = \frac{10 \div 2}{12 \div 2} = \frac{5}{6}$$

**Result**: $\frac{2}{3} \div \frac{4}{5} = \frac{5}{6}$.

**One more example**: You have $\frac{5}{6}$ of a pizza and want to divide it
into portions of $\frac{1}{3}$ each. How many portions?

$$\frac{5}{6} \div \frac{1}{3} = \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{3}{1} = \frac{5 \times 3}{6 \times 1} = \frac{15}{6}$$

Simplify. GCF of 15 and 6 is 3:

$$\frac{15}{6} = \frac{15 \div 3}{6 \div 3} = \frac{5}{2} = 2\frac{1}{2}$$

**Result**: You get $2\frac{1}{2}$ portions.

## Step 5: Multiplying mixed numbers

A recipe serves 4 people and calls for $1\frac{1}{2}$ cups of flour. You want to
serve 6 (that is $1\frac{1}{2}$ times the recipe).

1. Convert both mixed numbers to improper fractions:

$$1\frac{1}{2} = \frac{1 \times 2 + 1}{2} = \frac{3}{2} \quad \text{(the flour)}$$

$$1\frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{2} \quad \text{(the scaling factor)}$$

2. Multiply:

$$\frac{3}{2} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{3 \times 3}{2 \times 2} = \frac{9}{4}$$

3. Convert back: $9 \div 4 = 2$ remainder $1$.

$$\frac{9}{4} = 2\frac{1}{4}$$

**Result**: You need $2\frac{1}{4}$ cups of flour.

**Second example**: Multiply $2\frac{2}{3} \times 1\frac{1}{4}$.

1. Convert:

$$2\frac{2}{3} = \frac{2 \times 3 + 2}{3} = \frac{8}{3}$$

$$1\frac{1}{4} = \frac{1 \times 4 + 1}{4} = \frac{5}{4}$$

2. Multiply:

$$\frac{8}{3} \times \frac{5}{4} = \frac{8 \times 5}{3 \times 4} = \frac{40}{12}$$

3. Simplify. GCF of 40 and 12 is 4:

$$\frac{40}{12} = \frac{40 \div 4}{12 \div 4} = \frac{10}{3}$$

4. Convert back: $10 \div 3 = 3$ remainder $1$.

$$\frac{10}{3} = 3\frac{1}{3}$$

**Result**: $2\frac{2}{3} \times 1\frac{1}{4} = 3\frac{1}{3}$.

## Step 6: Dividing mixed numbers

You have $3\frac{3}{4}$ cups of batter and want to divide it equally among bowls
that each hold $1\frac{1}{4}$ cups. How many bowls can you fill?

1. Convert to improper fractions:

$$3\frac{3}{4} = \frac{3 \times 4 + 3}{4} = \frac{15}{4}$$

$$1\frac{1}{4} = \frac{1 \times 4 + 1}{4} = \frac{5}{4}$$

2. Divide by multiplying by the reciprocal:

$$\frac{15}{4} \div \frac{5}{4} = \frac{15}{4} \times \frac{4}{5} = \frac{15 \times 4}{4 \times 5} = \frac{60}{20}$$

3. Simplify:

$$\frac{60}{20} = \frac{60 \div 20}{20 \div 20} = \frac{3}{1} = 3$$

**Result**: You can fill exactly 3 bowls.

## What we accomplished

In this tutorial, we:

- Multiplied fractions by multiplying numerators and denominators straight across
  (e.g., $\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{3}$)
- Multiplied fractions by whole numbers
  (e.g., $3 \times \frac{3}{4} = \frac{9}{4} = 2\frac{1}{4}$)
- Learned what reciprocals are and verified that $\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{4}{3} = 1$
- Divided fractions using "invert and multiply"
  (e.g., $\frac{2}{3} \div \frac{4}{5} = \frac{5}{6}$)
- Multiplied mixed numbers by converting to improper fractions first
  (e.g., $2\frac{2}{3} \times 1\frac{1}{4} = 3\frac{1}{3}$)
- Divided mixed numbers using the same convert-then-invert approach
  (e.g., $3\frac{3}{4} \div 1\frac{1}{4} = 3$)
