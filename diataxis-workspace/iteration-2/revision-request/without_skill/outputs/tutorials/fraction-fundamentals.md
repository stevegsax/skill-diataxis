# Tutorial: Your First Steps with Fractions

In this tutorial, we will build a solid foundation with fractions. By the end, you
will be able to read and write fractions, identify their parts, create equivalent
fractions, simplify fractions to their lowest terms, and work with mixed numbers.

## What we will need

- A willingness to work through each step (no skipping ahead)
- Comfort with basic multiplication and division of whole numbers

## Step 1: The parts of a fraction

Let's start with a simple fraction:

```
  3
 ---
  4
```

Every fraction has two parts:

- **Numerator** (top number): tells us how many parts we have. Here, **3**.
- **Denominator** (bottom number): tells us how many equal parts make up the whole. Here, **4**.

We read this as "three-fourths" or "three quarters."

**Check your understanding**: In the fraction 5/8, the numerator is **5** and the
denominator is **8**. We have 5 parts out of 8 equal parts.

Here are more examples to make this concrete:

| Fraction | Numerator | Denominator | Read as              |
|----------|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| 1/2      | 1         | 2           | one-half             |
| 2/5      | 2         | 5           | two-fifths           |
| 7/10     | 7         | 10          | seven-tenths         |
| 11/16    | 11        | 16          | eleven-sixteenths    |

## Step 2: Reading fractions from a visual model

Imagine a pizza cut into 4 equal slices. You eat 1 slice.

```
  [ X ] [   ] [   ] [   ]
   ate   left   left  left
```

The fraction of pizza you ate is **1/4** (one-fourth).
The fraction remaining is **3/4** (three-fourths).

Now imagine a pizza cut into 8 equal slices. You eat 3 slices.

```
  [ X ] [ X ] [ X ] [   ] [   ] [   ] [   ] [   ]
```

The fraction you ate is **3/8**. The fraction remaining is **5/8**.

**Notice that** the denominator always matches the total number of equal pieces,
and the numerator counts how many pieces we are talking about.

Let's try one more. A chocolate bar is divided into 12 squares. You break off 5
squares:

- Fraction eaten: **5/12**
- Fraction remaining: **7/12**
- Check: 5 + 7 = 12, which equals the denominator. The parts always add up to the whole.

## Step 3: Equivalent fractions

Let's go back to our pizza. Take a pizza cut into 4 slices and eat 2 of them:

```
  [ X ] [ X ] [   ] [   ]       -->  2/4
```

Now imagine cutting each of those 4 slices in half, giving us 8 slices total.
The amount of pizza eaten has not changed, but now we can describe it differently:

```
  [X] [X] [X] [X] [ ] [ ] [ ] [ ]   -->  4/8
```

We have just discovered that **2/4 = 4/8**. These are **equivalent fractions** ---
different ways of writing the same amount.

**The rule**: We can create an equivalent fraction by multiplying (or dividing)
both the numerator and denominator by the same number.

Let's verify with real numbers:

```
2/4  -->  (2 x 2) / (4 x 2)  =  4/8    Correct.
```

Try another: starting from 1/3, multiply both parts by 4:

```
1/3  -->  (1 x 4) / (3 x 4)  =  4/12
```

So **1/3 = 4/12**.

Here is a chain of equivalent fractions for 1/2, each created by multiplying top
and bottom by the next whole number:

```
1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 = 4/8 = 5/10 = 6/12
```

Every one of these equals one-half. You can verify any pair --- for example,
3/6: multiply 1 by 3 to get 3, multiply 2 by 3 to get 6.

## Step 4: Simplifying fractions

Simplifying means finding the equivalent fraction with the smallest possible
numbers. We do this by dividing both parts by their greatest common factor (GCF).

**Example 1**: Simplify **6/8**.

1. Find the GCF of 6 and 8. Factors of 6: 1, 2, 3, 6. Factors of 8: 1, 2, 4, 8.
   The greatest common factor is **2**.

2. Divide both parts by 2:

```
6/8  -->  (6 / 2) / (8 / 2)  =  3/4
```

The simplified form of 6/8 is **3/4**.

**Example 2**: Simplify **10/15**.

1. GCF of 10 and 15: factors of 10 are 1, 2, 5, 10; factors of 15 are 1, 3, 5, 15.
   GCF is **5**.

2. Divide both by 5:

```
10/15  -->  (10 / 5) / (15 / 5)  =  2/3
```

So **10/15 = 2/3**.

**Example 3**: Simplify **18/24**.

1. Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18. Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24.
   GCF is **6**.

2. Divide both by 6:

```
18/24  -->  (18 / 6) / (24 / 6)  =  3/4
```

So **18/24 = 3/4**.

**How to tell a fraction is fully simplified**: the numerator and denominator
share no common factor other than 1. For 3/4, the factors of 3 are 1 and 3; the
factors of 4 are 1, 2, and 4. They share only 1, so 3/4 is fully simplified.

## Step 5: Mixed numbers and improper fractions

So far, every fraction we have seen has a numerator smaller than its denominator
(like 3/4 or 5/8). These are called **proper fractions** --- they represent less
than one whole.

But what if you eat 5 slices from a pizza cut into 4? That is more than one whole
pizza. We write this as the **improper fraction** 5/4 (numerator larger than
denominator).

A **mixed number** combines a whole number with a proper fraction. The improper
fraction 5/4 as a mixed number is **1 1/4** --- that is, 1 whole pizza plus 1/4
of another.

### Converting an improper fraction to a mixed number

Convert **11/3** to a mixed number:

1. Divide the numerator by the denominator: 11 / 3 = **3** remainder **2**
2. The quotient (3) is the whole number part
3. The remainder (2) goes over the original denominator: 2/3

```
11/3 = 3 2/3
```

Another example --- convert **17/5**:

1. 17 / 5 = **3** remainder **2**
2. Result: **3 2/5**

```
17/5 = 3 2/5
```

### Converting a mixed number to an improper fraction

Convert **2 3/4** to an improper fraction:

1. Multiply the whole number by the denominator: 2 x 4 = 8
2. Add the numerator: 8 + 3 = 11
3. Put that over the original denominator: 11/4

```
2 3/4 = (2 x 4 + 3) / 4 = 11/4
```

Another example --- convert **5 1/6**:

1. 5 x 6 = 30
2. 30 + 1 = 31
3. Result: **31/6**

```
5 1/6 = (5 x 6 + 1) / 6 = 31/6
```

### Real-world mixed numbers

Mixed numbers come up constantly in everyday life:

- A board is **2 3/8** feet long (2 feet plus 3/8 of a foot)
- A recipe calls for **1 1/2** cups of flour
- A child is **3 3/4** years old
- A drive takes **1 1/4** hours

In all these cases, you have some number of wholes plus a fractional part.

## What we accomplished

In this tutorial, we:

- Identified the numerator and denominator as the two parts of every fraction
- Read and wrote fractions from visual models
- Created equivalent fractions by multiplying both parts by the same number
- Simplified fractions to lowest terms by dividing both parts by their GCF
- Converted between mixed numbers and improper fractions
